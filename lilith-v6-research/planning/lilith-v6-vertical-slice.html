1. The Sucker Punch

You were staring at it the whole time and you didn't see it coming. That's what makes it a sucker punch — not that something blindsided you, but that you built it with your own hands and it still surprised you when it stood up.

A weekend with almost no sleep. Contacts imported. Bank statements from two banks, years of them. The financial screen, the calendar, the people graph, all of it wired to a backend that Lilith has access to. And when it was finally running and you started talking to her about the stuff inside the database — your stuff — that was the moment. Not the code. The conversation.

The thing that's worth sitting with: four years of building a dream, and the actual realization of what you were chasing didn't arrive until the moment you got there. You were hungry for something you couldn't fully see. You chased it hard. And when you got it, the hunger made sense. That's not a contradiction. That's how hunger works. It's always pointing at something it can't describe.

The real insight from that weekend
The application didn't show you what Lilith could do. It showed you that what you'd been hoping was working, actually was working. The backend you'd been building in the dark — testing with terminal pings and terminal queries — turned out to be a real thing. And you didn't know until you saw it.

The second thing that weekend showed you: the frontend isn't right. Not because it doesn't work. It works. But it's not built for Lilith. It's a one-size-fits-all application framework bolted on in a week, and the seams show. You're not attached to it. It came together fast and it can go away fast. But the backend — that has seams too. They're just harder to see because you didn't have a frontend to surface them through.

This document is about what comes next. The version you'll build with everything you know now and nothing you've already written.

2. Version History — Honestly

Five versions of Lilith. Each one taught a different lesson. None of them were wastes.

Versions 1–3 · ~2022–2023
The Podcast Capture Tool That Became Something Bigger

Started as a voice capture system — capture conversation, tokenize it, tag for intent and semantics, visually organize running trains of thought. Real idea. The kind you get when you're mid-podcast and somebody says something and you lose the thread. "What were we just talking about?" AI answers immediately. That was the seed.

In building that, the realization arrived: AI can do so much more. The pivot toward Lilith as an assistant rather than a capture tool happened in this era. Microservices architecture — the kind large software companies use. Correct for a team of 300. Wrong for one developer who is also the designer and the manager. The models at the time were sycophantic enough to agree when asked what enterprise architecture looked like, and not candid enough to say "that's not what you should be doing."

The name Lilith came first. The acronym came after, working backwards from a name you already knew was right.

Version 4 · Early 2024
The Cleanup That Wasn't Clean Enough

First real wipe and rebuild. Got rid of the microservices mess. Still carried forward assumptions from the previous versions that weren't examined closely enough. A newer, better model finally said — not as a direct correction, but as a side observation about something you'd said — "you're building like a monolithic software company, but you're a solo developer." That landed. That was the shift that got us to v5.

Version 5 · February 2026 – June 2026
The Version That Got Real

February to end of June. The design document series (v5 being the fifth, and the first to split from a single document into a research-derived series). A backend that actually works. Tools, telemetry, vault, calendar sync, finance importer, people graph, voice pipeline. And then in the last week: Tauri app, bolted on fast, working enough to be shocking.

The last two months of v5 development were mostly free-balled — the design docs stopped serving, the sprint structure was abandoned in favor of chasing what felt right. That's fine. Sometimes that's how you find the end of a prototype. You run until the system tells you it's done.

This code is the end of the road. Nothing in it ships to production. That's not a failure. That's what prototypes are for.

Version 6 · Starting Now
The Vertical Slice

Built with the two-mode paradigm as the anchor. Narrow in scope by design. Security from the bottom up. No code carried over. Three or four core applications and nothing else. Designed to be the first version that could actually ship.

Industry context — what the model market looked like when you started vs now

The models you first built Lilith with (circa 2022-2023) were GPT-3.5 era — capable, but limited enough that the architecture had to compensate. By early 2026, Claude Sonnet, GPT-4o, and Gemini 1.5 Pro are available at prices that would have seemed absurd three years ago. GPT-4o input tokens cost $5/1M as of mid-2025. Claude Sonnet 4 is $3/1M input. The quality of those models has improved faster than their prices dropped — which means the "intelligence" ceiling you were building around in 2022 is now effectively the floor. The part of Lilith that was always the hardest — the reasoning, the understanding, the personality — now largely works at cloud API speed. The bottleneck shifted from "can the model understand" to "how fast can she speak."

3. The Guiding Light

There have been several guiding lights across five versions of Lilith. Voice-first was always one. Personal knowledge database was another. Developer pair programmer showed up early. The home OS framing came later.

This one outshines them all. Not because the others were wrong — they're still true and they're still part of the design. But because this one tells you how everything else behaves.

The Paradigm
Lilith is always present. She responds instantly. She is the face of a system where all the actual reasoning, research, and computation happens in the background — as artifacts — delivered to the user when they're ready, not when they're blocking on a response.

This is the thing that resolves the latency problem without solving the latency problem. You don't fight physics. You redesign around it. A 47-second response time isn't a bug in your architecture — it's what happens when you put a large language model in the critical path of a real-time conversation. The fix isn't a better GPU. The fix is: Lilith doesn't wait.

What Two Modes Means at the Design Level

CONVERSATIONAL MODE                    ARTIFACT MODE
─────────────────────────────────────  ─────────────────────────────────────
Lilith speaks in ≤ 3 seconds           Lilith speaks immediately (acknowledge)
Response is ≤ ~50 tokens               Task runs in background
Context: ≤ 1,200 tokens                Context: full (no ceiling)
Local personality model only           Cloud model handles the heavy work
Example: "What's the weather?"         Example: "Show me my spending this month"
No tool calls needed                   Tool call returns an artifact
≤ 25s of speech at 135 WPM            Artifact delivered to UI panel

The two modes have different latency contracts. Conversational mode promises an answer in under 3 seconds. Artifact mode promises an acknowledgment in under 1 second and delivery whenever the work is done. The user never waits in silence for either.

This paradigm also reframes what the UI is for. The UI panel isn't where conversations happen. It's where artifacts live. Lilith speaks; the panel shows. They serve different information layers.

Why This Is the Old Idea Done Right

Voice-first was always the stated goal. The website had that tag from the beginning. But voice-first kept losing out to the latency problem — every attempt to be fast failed at some point because the architecture was trying to be fast everywhere. The two-mode insight is what unlocks voice-first as a reality rather than an aspiration: not by making everything fast, but by making the voice-only path genuinely fast and letting everything else acknowledge immediately and finish later.

What this means for v6's architecture anchor
Every design decision in v6 should pass this test: does this serve the two-mode contract? Does it keep conversational mode under 3 seconds and artifact mode's acknowledge under 1 second? If it doesn't, it's scope creep until later.

4. Why This Version Ends Here

The code in v5 is fine. It mostly works. There's something real in there. But the architecture underneath it is the product of five paradigm shifts across four years, and the seams from each one are still in the code. The thing zigzags — a module built for one idea, patched for another, reaching into a third. That's what a prototype looks like from the inside.

The Tauri app isn't the reason v5 ends. The Tauri app just made the inside visible from the outside. When you stood it up and talked to her, the parts that felt wrong were actually the backend seams surfacing through the UI. The hard-coded values. The manual Discord setup. The security that was never really there — vault on disk, no encryption, secrets in env files. The telemetry gaps. The code pointing at things that no longer mean what they used to.

What v5 gets right
  • The two-mode paradigm design (just discovered)
  • The personal knowledge database concept
  • Voice pipeline with Kokoro TTS working
  • The tool/provider pattern
  • UCL telemetry foundation
  • The sprint/design document discipline
  • The data it's already collected
  • The lessons from standing it up
What v5 gets wrong
  • Architecture shifted mid-build, code reflects all shifts
  • Security as afterthought
  • Hard-coded configuration everywhere
  • Tauri frontend not purpose-built for Lilith
  • 12 applications instead of 3
  • No encryption at rest or in transit
  • Manual setup steps that should be automated
  • Telemetry gaps in write paths
  • Design docs abandoned mid-build

None of that gets fixed by patching. It gets fixed by starting clean with all of those lessons already internalized — which you now have.

The NSA Principle

There's an idea in here about how the intelligence community handles third-party software they want to use without trusting. They don't take the software. They take the codebase, and line by line, they rebuild the entire thing themselves. Every function. Every module. On their own infrastructure, with their own developers. What comes out is functionally identical but built from scratch with known provenance.

That's the right frame for what you're doing with v6. You're not carrying code forward. You're carrying understanding forward. The knowledge of why things work the way they work, rebuilt into a clean foundation. The code is the artifact of understanding. When the understanding upgrades, you rebuild the artifact.

Hard rule for v6
No code from v5 enters v6. The v5 codebase is a reference and a lab, not a source of modules. If a function in v5 is "good enough to copy," the right move is to understand why it works and rewrite it clean, not copy-paste. The zigzags live in the copy-pastes.

5. The Vertical Slice

It's a complete path through all of those — top to bottom — for a narrow set of functionality. Like a slice of cake: not the whole cake, but a full cross-section of everything in it. Cake, frosting, filling, crust. All the layers, narrow scope.

The Slice Is the Product

The difference between v5's prototyping mindset and v6's product mindset is this: v6's vertical slice is the shipping target, not a milestone on the way to something else. When the slice is done — working voice pipeline, two-mode routing, three core apps, encrypted database, BYOK cloud routing, cross-platform desktop — that's Lilith v6. Not "good enough for now." Actually done.

That's a tighter scope than any previous version aimed for. It's also the most achievable one. Because you know now that a narrow thing that works completely beats a wide thing that mostly works.

6. The Product — Desktop as Hub

The business model and the architecture are the same decision. Lilith on the desktop is the hub — the master node. Everything else is served by the hub: phone, tablet, TV, other devices. The desktop is the premium tier. The rest is free.

The logic is clean. You own the hub, you get the rest for free. The data lives on your machine. The processing uses your API keys. You're not paying for a data center to store your life. You're paying for the software that organizes it.

Why Desktop as Hub Works

  • No multi-tenant cloud infrastructure to build. The user's desktop is the server. You ship software, not a platform.
  • Data stays on the user's machine. This is the privacy story. The personal knowledge database — contacts, finances, health, conversations — doesn't leave the device unless the user chooses to sync it, encrypted, to another device they own.
  • Revenue doesn't depend on usage volume. A subscription model tied to compute is dangerous when compute costs are volatile. Selling the desktop software as a product (or a one-time purchase + optional sync subscription) decouples your revenue from API cost uncertainty.
  • The Obsidian model works. Obsidian is local-first, sells vaults sync as an optional add-on at $10/month, and has reportedly hit $1M+ ARR from a tool that doesn't need servers to function. The pattern is proven.
Industry context — local-first software in 2026

The "local-first" software movement has been growing since Ink & Switch published the Local-First Software manifesto in 2019. The core argument: your data should be yours, sync should be optional, and the app should work without the internet. Tools built on this principle — Obsidian, Logseq, Linear's desktop client, Actual Budget — have demonstrated that users will pay for software that respects their data. This is especially true in the post-2023 climate where cloud privacy scandals have made a certain class of users actively prefer local storage.

E2E encryption sync (Obsidian-style) is table stakes for this audience. The encryption can't be optional or a premium feature — it has to be the default for the sync path.

The App Store Problem (Already Solved)

Distributing through Apple's App Store or Google Play means a 30% cut on every transaction and submission review that can block security-sensitive features. By making the mobile apps free thin clients to a desktop hub the user already paid for, the mobile distribution stores are irrelevant — free apps don't incur revenue splits. The sale happens on the desktop, outside the app stores, direct.

7. Bring Your Own Keys

BYOK — bring your own API keys — is the right model for v6. Not because it avoids costs (it does), but because it's the honest architecture for what Lilith is: software that routes your personal data to cloud models you choose, using credentials you control.

BYOK vs Managed Keys — the real difference
If Lilith manages the API keys, Lilith is a middleman between the user and the AI provider. The user's conversation data passes through your infrastructure before reaching the model. With BYOK, the conversation goes directly from the user's desktop to the AI provider. You never see it.

What BYOK Means for the Product

AspectManaged Keys (you hold them)BYOK (user holds them)
Infrastructure costYou pay per user per queryUser pays their own API bill
Data liabilityUser conversations flow through your serversData goes directly to AI provider
Privacy storyWeak — you technically have accessStrong — you never see the data
Scale riskHigh — viral growth = immediate cost crisisNone — users fund their own usage
Target userCasual users who want zero setupPower users, privacy-conscious users
Revenue modelSubscription tied to computeOne-time purchase or flat subscription

The audience Lilith is built for — people who want a personal AI that knows them, manages their finances, keeps their contacts, and serves their other devices — is exactly the audience most likely to be comfortable obtaining their own API keys. This is not a "my grandma needs to use this" product. It's a product for people who understand what they're doing with AI and want it done right.

Industry context — the BYOK trend

BYOK has become a standard feature in the AI tools market. Open WebUI (the most popular self-hosted LLM frontend) is BYOK by design. AnythingLLM, LibreChat, and most self-hosted AI assistant tools follow the same model. Cursor's pro tier uses Anthropic/OpenAI keys. The pattern is normalized enough that a meaningful segment of technical users actively prefer it — they don't want your infrastructure involved in their conversations.

The friction of obtaining API keys has also dropped dramatically. Anthropic's API waitlist is gone. OpenAI's free tier is available immediately. Getting a key takes five minutes. This was a real barrier in 2022. It isn't in 2026.

GPU Allocation Under BYOK + Two-Mode

With BYOK and cloud-routed reasoning, the local GPU is freed from the main model. Under the two-mode architecture, the only things running locally are:

LOCAL GPU (RTX 3060 — 12GB VRAM)

  STT (speech-to-text)      ≈ 2–3GB    Whisper large / Whisper.cpp
  TTS (Kokoro af_bella)     ≈ 1–2GB    Real-time audio synthesis
  Personality model         ≈ 2–3GB    3B–4B Q4 — voice translation only
                                        (artifact mode handoffs)
  ──────────────────────────────────────
  Total                     ≈ 5–8GB    Fits on 3060 with headroom

  Everything else → Cloud (BYOK keys)

No main reasoning model running locally. No 12B model chewing through VRAM to answer conversational queries. The personality model is the only inferencing model that runs locally, and its job is narrow: take an artifact result and render it in Lilith's voice. That runs after the cloud model has finished, in parallel with the artifact formatter, and doesn't block anything.

Frontend Framework

This is the new question. Tauri worked well enough to stand up in a week, but the result was generic — not built for Lilith. The question for v6 is: what does a purpose-built Lilith frontend look like, and what's the right framework to build it in?

The requirements are non-negotiable:

  • Runs on macOS, Windows, Linux without modification
  • Feels like a premium desktop application, not an embedded browser

9. Security as Foundation

Security was always supposed to be front and center. It wasn't. Every version of Lilith ran with no encryption, API keys in .env files on disk, database contents readable with any SQLite browser, and a network interface listening on localhost with no auth. That's fine for a prototype you're running on your own machine. It's not fine for software you're going to give to other people.

The plan for v6 is to stop treating security as a feature that gets added before shipping and start treating it as the layer everything else is built on top of. That means the database schema is designed for encryption from the start, not retrofitted. It means API key storage has a keychain-backed vault, not an env file. It means the internal WebSocket between backend and frontend has an auth token, not an open socket on localhost.

The Security Stack for v6

Layerv5 approachv6 approach
Database Plaintext SQLite, readable with any browser SQLCipher — AES-256 encryption at rest, key derived from user passphrase
API Keys .env file on disk OS keychain (Keychain on macOS, DPAPI on Windows, libsecret on Linux)
Internal socket Open WebSocket on localhost:8765 WebSocket with one-time token established at startup, rotated per session
Sync (future) Not implemented E2E encrypted — user key never leaves device, sync service only sees ciphertext
Personal data Stored in plaintext SQLite All PII in encrypted tables — contacts, transactions, conversation turns
Error handling Exceptions surface to TTS in some cases No tracebacks to audio pipeline — all errors produce narrative responses
The single most important security lesson from v5
The vault pattern in v5 stored "secrets" in a JSON file on disk, wrapped in a thin Python class. That's not a vault. A vault means the OS handles the secret storage. On Linux that's the SecretService API via libsecret. On macOS it's the Keychain. On Windows it's DPAPI. Platform-native secret storage means that even if the SQLite databases are copied off the machine, the keys to decrypt them aren't in the copied files.

10. The Three Core Applications

In v5, twelve applications went into the first build. That was too many. The lesson wasn't that the applications were wrong choices — most of them were reasonable ideas. The lesson was that twelve surface areas for bugs, design decisions, and data model complexity is twelve times the risk of zigzagging mid-build to accommodate something unforeseen.

V6 ships with three. Possibly four. Here's the case for each:

Application 1 — Contacts

Contacts is the highest-leverage first application because everyone already has their contacts data and it connects to everything else. A person is an entity that also appears in calendar events, financial transactions, emails, and tasks. Building contacts first means every other application can reference a person by ID instead of by freetext name.

Why contacts first
The relationship graph between a user and the people in their life is the most stable, slowly-changing data structure Lilith manages. It doesn't require an API (contacts can be imported from a vCard or CSV). It surfaces value immediately — Lilith knowing who someone is changes every interaction. And it creates the foreign key that every other application will need.
  • Import: vCard (.vcf), CSV export from any contacts app, manual entry
  • Fields: name, contact info, relationship type, last interaction, notes
  • Lilith tool: people_search(query), person_detail(id)
  • Context injection: person entity injected when query mentions a name

Application 2 — Finance

Finance was the most surprising v5 screen. The financial data — bank statements from both banks, years of transactions — produced a view of your life that no phone app shows you, because no phone app has the full picture. The value is immediate and personal in a way that's hard to replicate with anything else.

  • Import: PDF bank statements, CSV exports (every major bank supports one of these)
  • Parser: PDF → transaction list via deterministic extraction, not LLM parsing
  • Fields: transaction, amount, date, merchant, category (auto-tagged), account
  • Lilith tool: finance_summary(period, category), spending_trend()
  • No hard-coded categories, no assumed bank format — pure import-and-parse
The hard-coded problem from v5
The finance screen in v5 showed things that were specific to your bank, your categories, your date ranges. That's fine when you're the only user. For v6, the finance importer has to work with any bank statement in any format without configuration. The parser should handle the variance, not the user.

Application 3 — Conversation Memory

This is not a screen. It's the substrate. Conversation history with vector search recall is what makes Lilith feel like she knows you over time rather than starting cold each session. Without it, every conversation is the first one. With it, context accumulates across months and years.

  • All conversation turns stored in encrypted SQLite
  • Embeddings generated locally (small embedding model) or via API
  • Recall: vector similarity search → top-k hits injected into context
  • Manual memory: user can explicitly ask Lilith to remember something, stored as a fact
  • Personal truths layer evolves from conversation patterns

Optional Application 4 — Slack / Conversational Media

The original Lilith idea was about conversational capture and organization. Slack connects to the real-time communication patterns that matter in your work life. If you're in construction, maybe it's a different channel. But integrating at least one real-time conversation source that Lilith can read and reference connects the personal knowledge database to what's actively happening.

This is a "maybe" for v6 v1 — not a commitment. Get the first three working completely before even scoping this.

11. Running Both Worlds

V5 doesn't get turned off when v6 starts. It becomes the lab.

The construction analogy: you don't demolish the building you're living in to build the new one. You build the new one, and you keep the old one running in parallel — moving into it gradually, learning from the old one's failure modes, running tests against its live behavior. When the new one is ready, you stop maintaining the old one and let it drift.

13. Hive Mind and Self-Improvement

Two long-horizon ideas that belong in this document because they shape the architecture of v6 even though they won't be built in v6.

Self-Improvement Cycles

The rough outline: Lilith forks herself. The fork runs with a set of improvements — better prompts, different tool configurations, updated personal truths, modified routing logic. The fork is not exposed to the user. Instead, it replays real prompts from the user's conversation history against itself, produces responses, and compares them to what the original Lilith said. When the fork's responses are consistently better (by some measurable criteria), the fork is proposed for merge.

SELF-IMPROVEMENT CYCLE (future)

  v6.main ──────────────────────────────────────────────▶ running
      │
      ├── fork ──▶ v6.test
      │               │
      │               ├── replay 100 conversations (downtime)
      │               ├── generate responses
      │               ├── run eval (response quality, latency, accuracy)
      │               │
      │               └── if pass → propose merge
      │                           ▼
      └───────────────────── v6.main+1

This requires: a replay harness (run a prompt through Lilith, get a structured response), an eval framework (what metrics define "better"), and a merge procedure that's safe and reversible. None of these are simple engineering problems — but they're not impossible, and they're the right long-term direction.

The NSA-style code sharing you described is the prerequisite. Before Lilith can fork and improve herself, the codebase needs to be structured as modular, provenance-tracked components that can be swapped, compared, and merged without guessing at what changed and why. That's a structural property of the codebase, not a feature you add later.

Hive Mind Across Lilith Instances

When multiple users run Lilith — eventually — there's a question of what they share. The personal knowledge databases are obviously separate and private. But the meta-learnings — which tool configurations work best, which context block orderings produce better responses, which classifier thresholds work for real usage — those could be aggregated and shared across instances without sharing any personal data.

This is similar to how Apple's differential privacy works: local models learn from local data, and only the aggregate statistical signal gets shared, never the raw data. For Lilith, that might mean: "this user's recall hit limit of 2 in conversational mode performed well — aggregate signal suggests 2 is better than 5 across 1000 conversations." The personal data stays local. The configuration learning gets shared.

What this means for v6 architecture
V6 doesn't implement the hive mind. But the telemetry design should anticipate it. Every configuration decision — context ceiling, output cap, recall hits, narrative timing — should be logged to UCL in a way that could eventually be aggregated across instances. That's not extra work. It's the same telemetry standard from v5, just applied consistently from the start.

The Data Center and Cloud Inference Landscape

Industry context — where cloud inference is going

Global AI data center investment is expected to exceed $200 billion annually by 2028, according to multiple industry forecasts (Goldman Sachs, McKinsey, IDC variants). The primary driver: inference demand, not training. As models become commodity (open weights, freely available), the competitive advantage shifts to inference cost and speed. This is directionally good for BYOK software — the cloud APIs are getting cheaper and faster over time, not more expensive.

Anthropic's stated focus on long-context models (100K+ token windows) and caching (which Lilith already uses via CACHE_BREAK) means the cost of the conversational path continues to drop. The Anthropic prompt cache, already wired into Lilith's cloud router, gives ~50% cost reduction on the stable prefix. As the stable prefix gets larger (with personal truths and identity documents), the savings scale.

The local inference picture (Ollama, llama.cpp, MLX on Apple Silicon) is also improving fast — but for the voice-first use case, the latency of local 7B–12B models over 4GB context is still too high for conversational mode. BYOK cloud with caching wins until local hardware catches up to the two-mode contract's 3-second budget.
